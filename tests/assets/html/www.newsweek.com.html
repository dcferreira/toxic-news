Russian Opposition’s Garry Kasparov Predicts Collapse of Russian 'Empire'

TL/DR: "This is the process that stopped in 1991 with the collapse of the Soviet Union, but I think the collapse of the Russian empire is imminent,” Russian opposition figure Garry Kasparov told Newsweek.

Russian opposition figure Garry Kasparov said that Ukraine winning the war would lead to the collapse of President Vladimir Putin's regime and usher in a new start for Russia. "I'm pretty sure that within the next five years, Russia will end up smaller," the former world chess champion told Newsweek. He described the war as a "genocide," the consequences of which "will be with us for decades." Russian territories such as the republics of Tatarstan, Bashkortostan and Chechnya "very likely will walk away."

Kasparov believes Ukraine should be given the needed weapons to win as it would end Putin's regime when "we still have some staples of the Russian governing mechanism that could be brought into a new system of governance." Kasparov noted that many of the estimated 900,000 Russians who have left the country since the war broke out were upper middle class and bringing them together might "create a staple for the future Russian elite."

What happens now? In building this new Russia, Kasparov said the West should not make the mistakes of the aftermath of the Iraq war in which ended in “total chaos." After the war, there needs to be justice for those closely involved, but they need to be distinguished from other Russian officials, Kasparov said, noting that 90% of “them are not war criminals.” Opponents of Putin who cannot leave the country are left with few options amid a clampdown on human rights. Kasparov called these people "heroes" and insisted "the moment will come when the system will collapse, and then we need these people. We need this anger."

Kaja Kallas, Europe's New 'Iron Lady'

TL/DR: "I don't know how to answer this," Kallas told Newsweek, laughing. "I guess it shows that I've been very firm about the issues that I'm talking about. And I think it is meant as a compliment.”

Estonia's Prime Minister Kaja Kallas has emerged as one of Europe's most combative leaders as the continent grapples with the return of major war, with the PM being referred to in the West as the "new Iron Lady" of Europe. Kallas, who took office in 2021, has been among those in Europe urging a hardline response to Russian aggression in Ukraine. The leader made Estonia the first country to send lethal aid to Ukraine prior to the invasion and became one of a minority of NATO members to increase military spending to above 2% of GDP.

"Russia is a pariah state that needs to be isolated," Kallas said in Copenhagen this week. "There's no room for flirtation with the idea of resuming business as usual with Russia. Our joint pressure against Russia must increase, not decrease." But her public mandate in this year's election has not prevented a deadlock in the Estonian parliament over proposed cuts to family benefits and tax hikes, which the ruling coalition says are needed to fund the country's rearmament.

What happens now? Far from merely upholding Estonia’s supportive role to more “prominent” European Union and NATO members, Kallas appears keen to put the small country in a position of power. In an interview with Newsweek, Kallas said “one lesson from this war” is that Western European nations “should have listened to the voices inside our union—in Poland, in the Baltics, and all across Central and Eastern Europe,” who were warning that a Russian attack was imminent.

San Francisco Naming First Drag Laureate Sparks Huge Debate

TL/DR: The National Center for Lesbian Rights called the drag laureate position a “shining light of hope and joy" during a time when “drag queens are being targeted by legislative attacks and actual threats of physical harm."

San Francisco has come under fire after announcing the appointment of its first "drag laureate", prompting an online debate about the city's use of public funds, as well as about drag culture as a whole. Drag queen D'Arcy Drollinger will receive a $55,000 stipend paid by the San Francisco Public Library over an 18-month term.

Proponents praise the scheme for promoting drag and LGBTQ+ inclusion at a time when the culture is under attack. Fourteen state legislatures are currently considering bills to restrict or ban drag shows, and a moral panic centered on "drag queen story time" events has been intensifying over the last year. But those criticizing the appointment, including many who are pro-drag culture, believe it represents an inappropriate use of funds in a city undergoing a rampant homelessness, crime and drug death crisis.

What happens now? As drag laureate, Drollinger’s role will include hosting and participating in drag events, as well as serving as a spokesperson for San Francisco’s LGBTQ+ community. Drollinger believes the establishment of the role “telegraphs to the rest of the country that drag is not something to be scared of.” But the wider community still faces an uphill battle across the U.S., with the American Civil Liberties Union currently tracking 474 anti-LGBTQ+ pieces of legislation around the country, including Tennessee’s unprecedented strict law that essentially bans drag from public spaces.

Book Bans in Florida Violate First Amendment, Lawsuit Alleges

TL/DR: The lawsuit alleges the school district is “depriving students of access to a wide range of viewpoints” through the book removals.

A new lawsuit accuses a Florida school district of violating the First Amendment over banning certain books about race and LGBTQ+ issues from classrooms. Free expression nonprofit PEN America, publisher Penguin Random House and a handful of authors and parents are suing the Escambia County School District over the removal of 10 books. "Censorship in the form of book bans" are a "direct threat to democracy and our Constitutional rights," Penguin Random House CEO Nihar Malaviya said.

Book bans are soaring across the U.S.; an April PEN America report found a 28% increase between the first and second halves of 2022, most stories involve stories about LGBTQ+ issues and people of color. Florida is one of five states where the nonprofit says book bans are "most prevalent," and Governor Ron DeSantis has backed many such efforts ahead of his anticipated presidential campaign launch. While free speech advocates argue against book bans, DeSantis has said Florida teachers are merely removing "pornographic and inappropriate materials" from classrooms.

What happens now? PEN America CEO Suzanne Nossel said the law “demands” the school district “put removed or restricted books back on library shelves where they belong.” Polling suggests most parents are concerned about book bans. Meanwhile, DeSantis reportedly plans to file presidential campaign paperwork next week. Two DeSantis-signed anti-LGBTQ+ bills regulating some public bathroom use and pronouns in schools take effect in July; two others banning minors from receiving gender-affirming care and from attending drag shows are already in place.

El Niño is Brewing and Could Cost Trillions

TL/DR: El Nino’s costs exceed previously estimated numbers, with new assessments revealing the weather phenomenon amounting to trillions of dollars in damages.

Artificial intelligence, or AI, might be able to generate its own artwork, prose, and even music, but it also might be able to replace your romantic partners.

One influencer has found a way to use AI to become the girlfriend of over 1,000 people, using a voice-based chatbot that is a near-clone of herself.

Caryn Marjorie, a 23-year-old influencer with 1.8 million followers on Snapchat, charges her followers up to $1 per minute to interact with her digital self, CarynAI. Trained by thousands of hours of recordings of the real Marjorie, AI company Forever Voices' CarynAI is capable of mimicking her to a convincing degree, playing the role of virtual girlfriend to Marjorie's customers by chatting about future plans, sharing intimate feelings and sometimes even flirting sexually.

What happens now? The approaching 2023 El Niño is expected sometime between May and July, according to the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, and will continue through the winter. The event could bring intense flooding, crop loss, and slow economic growth, with lower-income nations closer to the phenomenon receiving the brunt of the impact. Research predicts this year's El Niño temperatures to exceed the last major one in 2016.
